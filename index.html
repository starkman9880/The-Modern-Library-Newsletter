“Reflection and discrimination seem to have been almost completely divorced from publishing. . . . Some of the largest houses in the country devote themselves chiefly to merchanting garbage that should make any self-respecting publisher blush.”

That was H. L. Mencken writing in May 1917. However, in the same month, enterprising publishers Horace Liveright and Albert Boni began printing small leatherette-bound editions of previously published books and selling them for sixty cents each under the name The Modern Library. The books were well received; Publishers Weekly said at the time, “The bookseller looking for a quick turnover will do well to look out for the Modern Library.” There was one problem, however: “When the warmer days of summer arrived, the books began to give off an unmistakably fishy odor, which was traced to the fish oil used in the bindings.”*

The Modern Library has now entered its second century. Our books are no longer sixty cents, unfortunately, but on the other hand they no longer smell of fish and if you choose to you can read them on your phone. What would Mr. Mencken make of that?

*We found this story in Tom Dardis’s Firebrand: The Life of Horace Liveright.
